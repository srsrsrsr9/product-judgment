There's a particular kind of document that exists in almost every product organization on earth. It has a stock photo at the top — usually a smiling person holding a coffee cup or staring thoughtfully at a laptop. Below the photo is a name that sounds like it was generated by a corporate random name picker: "Marketing Mary" or "Developer Dave." Then come the demographics: age 34, lives in Austin, household income $95,000, uses iPhone, drinks oat milk.

This document is called a "persona." And in most organizations, it is completely useless.

Not because personas are a bad idea — they're one of the best ideas in product development. But because most personas are built wrong. They're demographic collages instead of human portraits. They describe *who* someone is on paper without capturing *what keeps them up at night*. They're the product equivalent of a dating profile: technically accurate, emotionally vacant, and missing everything that matters.

This chapter is about building personas that actually drive decisions. And about a complementary framework — Jobs To Be Done — that reveals what your personas are *hiring* your product to do.

<QuoteBlock quote="People don't want to buy a quarter-inch drill. They want a quarter-inch hole." attribution="Theodore Levitt, Harvard Business School" footnote="1" />

Actually, they don't want the hole either. They want the shelf that goes on the wall. And they want the shelf because their books are on the floor. And their books are on the floor because they just moved. The deeper you go, the closer you get to the real job.

## What's Wrong with Most Personas

Let's diagnose the disease before we prescribe the cure.

### The Demographic Trap

Here's a persona you might find in a typical product document:

```text
PERSONA: "HEALTH-CONSCIOUS HARRY"
==================================

Age: 35
Location: Bangalore, India
Income: ₹15-20 LPA
Education: Bachelor's in Engineering
Tech Savvy: High
Uses: iPhone, WhatsApp, Google Calendar
Hobbies: Cricket, fitness tracking
Marital Status: Married

Goal: "Wants to manage family health better"

───────────────────

What this tells you about what to build: Nothing.
What this tells you about design decisions: Nothing.
What this tells you about emotional state: Nothing.
What this tells you about why someone would
open your app at 3 AM: Nothing.
```

This persona describes a census category, not a human being. You could hand this to five different product teams and they'd build five completely different products — all of them wrong, because the persona gives you demographics without direction.

<QuoteBlock quote="A persona without a story is a mannequin. You can dress it up, but it won't tell you where it wants to go." attribution="adapted from Alan Cooper" footnote="2" />

## The "Everyone" Problem

The second failure mode is trying to build one persona that represents all your users. "Our persona is a 25-55 year old professional who uses smartphones." That's not a persona. That's a description of most adults on earth.

The whole point of personas is to exclude. When you say "we're building for Sandeep," you're also saying "we're not building for the doctor, the insurance company, the hospital administrator, or the teenager who Googles symptoms at midnight." Exclusion is the mechanism by which personas create focus. Without it, they're just decoration.

## The Wishful Thinking Problem

The third failure: building the persona you wish you had instead of the one who showed up in your research.

You want your user to be tech-savvy, data-driven, and eager to adopt new tools. Your actual user keeps a medication list taped to a refrigerator, has tried five health apps and abandoned all of them, and doesn't fully trust digital solutions even though they work in IT. That's not a design flaw in your user. That's the design constraint for your product.

## Building Personas That Work

A good persona does three things:

**Makes you feel something.** If you read the persona and don't feel an emotional pull — empathy, urgency, recognition — it's not finished.

**Constrains decisions.** Every design choice should be answerable with "would this work for [persona name]?" If the persona is too vague to answer that question, it's useless.

**Comes from research, not imagination.** Every claim in the persona should trace back to an interview. If you can't cite where an insight came from, it's a guess wearing a persona's clothes.

### The Anatomy of a Real Persona

Let me show you what this looks like in practice. Here are four personas from the family health app — all drawn from the research described in Chapters 2 and 3.<a class="fn-ref" href="#fn-3">3</a>

### SANDEEP KUMAR — "The Local Coordinator"

**~40% of target users**

Sandeep is 29. He's an IT professional in Visakhapatnam who works from home three days a week. He lives with his parents. His father, 62, has diabetes. His mother, 60, has high blood pressure. Sandeep himself was recently diagnosed with NAFLD — he's now managing three people's health conditions.

He's the family's default health manager. He books appointments, accompanies his parents to doctors, manages medications, maintains records, and makes decisions during emergencies. His parents are capable but prefer him to handle "the complicated stuff."

**What his day looks like:** He keeps a physical file of medical documents at home. He takes photos of every prescription — his phone gallery is a chaotic archive of medication labels and blood test reports. He blocks his calendar weeks in advance for medical appointments. Before every doctor visit, he spends 30+ minutes searching through WhatsApp messages, old photos, and physical files to piece together the relevant medical history.

**What keeps him up at night:** "The fear of forgetting scares me. Sometimes it's hard to remember all the data, the dos and don'ts. I must be attentive and readily available for them."

**What he's already tried:** He maintains a medication list taped to the refrigerator. He's tried one or two health apps but found the data entry tedious. He still keeps physical files as "backup" — he doesn't fully trust digital solutions.

**The product decision this drives:** Sandeep won't tolerate complex setup. If onboarding takes more than five minutes, he's gone — back to the refrigerator list. Every feature must save him more time than it costs to use. And "AI-generated" content must be editable, because he doesn't trust systems he can't override.

### PRIYA MEHTA — "The Distance Caregiver"

**~30% of target users**

Priya is 34. She's a software engineer in San Francisco. Her parents live in Visakhapatnam, India — a 12-hour time difference and 8,000 miles away. Her father has diabetes. Her mother has high blood pressure and ILD. Priya coordinates major healthcare decisions from abroad, booking appointments through relatives, reviewing test reports via WhatsApp photos, and consulting with doctors by phone.

She flies home once or twice a year, during which she tries to organize everything. She relies heavily on her younger brother for day-to-day coordination — and feels guilty about it.

**What her worst moment looks like:** A 3 AM phone call. Her father is in pain. She's scrolling through fifty WhatsApp messages trying to find his medication list while twelve time zones away. Her anxiety: 10 out of 10.

**What she's already tried:** Google Drive folder with scanned reports (organized by her brother). Screenshots of prescriptions from WhatsApp. Medication lists in the Notes app. She's tried 2-3 health apps — all abandoned because maintaining them from abroad was tedious.

**Her quote:** "What happens to my family when I'm not there? That fear haunts me."

**The product decision this drives:** Priya needs asynchronous access. She can't be on the phone during every appointment. She needs to review what happened — the briefing, the prescription, the follow-up tasks — on her own time, possibly while her parents are asleep. The product must work across time zones without requiring real-time coordination.

### ALEKHYA PATEL — "The Sandwich Juggler"

**~20% of target users**

Alekhya is 38. She's a marketing manager at a pharmaceutical company in Mumbai. Married, twin seven-year-olds (her son has autism requiring 3x weekly therapy), parents in a different city with chronic conditions. Her husband travels frequently for work.

She manages health for four people. She described it as "a second full-time job." She works during lunch breaks, coordinates care during evenings and weekends, and recently navigated both kids having bacterial infections simultaneously while her parents needed checkups.

**What keeps her up at night:** "Distraction, being overwhelmed, tired. I'm constantly thinking about everything I have to do and ask in the next appointment. What happens to my family when I'm no more?"

**What she's already tried:** Separate WhatsApp chats for kids' health and parents' health. Google Calendar with color coding. Photos of everything. Premium calendar apps. Dozens of phone reminders. She has tried 5-6 different health apps. Abandoned all of them.

**The product decision this drives:** Alekhya has zero patience for complexity. She's tried everything. She'll give your product about ninety seconds before she decides whether it's worth her time. The app must work while she's multitasking — which means voice input, minimal typing, and a flow that doesn't punish interruptions. If she gets pulled away mid-task (which she will), the app must save her progress without losing data.

### RAKESH SHARMA — "The Reluctant Manager"

**~10% of target users**

Rakesh is 26. He recently moved back to his hometown after his father had a health scare. He's suddenly in charge of health coordination for the first time — and he has no systems, no context, and no idea what he's doing.

He doesn't know which doctor treats which condition. He doesn't know where the old prescriptions are. He doesn't know what questions to ask. He's earnest, anxious, and operating entirely on fear and goodwill.

**His quote:** He doesn't have a pithy quote yet because we only found two Rakesh-types in our research. But here's what he represents: the entry point. The person who Googles "how to manage parent health" and discovers your product because they're starting from absolute zero.

**The product decision this drives:** Rakesh is the onboarding test case. If the app can guide a complete beginner through their first doctor visit cycle — from "I don't even know what information I need" to "I walked in prepared and walked out with a plan" — it can serve anyone. Design the guided first-use experience for Rakesh. Everyone else will benefit.

## Personas Are Not Enough (Enter: Jobs To Be Done)

Personas tell you **who**. They don't tell you **why**.

Sandeep is a 29-year-old IT professional who lives with his parents. That's who he is. But why does he open your app? What's the trigger? What outcome is he hiring your product to achieve?

This is where Jobs To Be Done comes in.<a class="fn-ref" href="#fn-4">4</a>

The JTBD framework was developed by Clayton Christensen and refined by Bob Moesta. The core insight is simple and revolutionary: people don't buy products. They hire products to do a job. And the job isn't about the product's features — it's about the progress the person is trying to make in their life.

The famous example: people don't buy a milkshake because they want a milkshake. Christensen's research found that a huge percentage of milkshakes were sold before 8 AM to commuters who needed something to make a boring drive more interesting and keep them full until lunch. The milkshake was being hired for the job of "make my commute less boring and keep me from getting hungry." Its competition wasn't other milkshakes — it was bananas, bagels, and boredom.<a class="fn-ref" href="#fn-5">5</a>

### The Structure of a Job

A job statement has a specific structure:

```text
JOBS TO BE DONE — FORMAT
═════════════════════════

"When [situation/trigger],
 help me [desired outcome]
 so I feel [emotional result]."

The situation is when the job arises.
The outcome is what success looks like.
The emotion is why they care.

All three matter. Skip the emotion and
you'll build a tool. Include it and
you'll build something people love.
```

### Five Jobs from Real Research

Here are the five critical jobs that emerged from the family health research. Notice: they're phrased from the user's perspective, in the user's emotional language, not in product-speak.

**Job 1: The 3 AM Emergency**

"When I get a panicked call at 3 AM from my parent saying something is wrong, help me sound competent and informed instead of terrified and useless."

This is the highest-stakes job. It doesn't happen often, but when it happens, the failure cost is catastrophic — both medically and emotionally. The current hires (scrolling WhatsApp, calling siblings) all fail because information is fragmented and inaccessible under pressure.

**Success looks like:** brief an ER doctor on complete medical history in under 2 minutes. Anxiety drops from 9-10 to 3-4.

**Job 2: The Remote Visit**

"When I can't attend a critical doctor visit due to distance or work, help me ensure nothing gets missed without requiring me to be on the phone the entire time."

This is Priya's primary job. The current hires — brother WhatsApp-updating live, pre-appointment lists in notes apps — fail because they require real-time coordination across time zones.

**Success looks like:** 5-minute prep instead of 45-minute scramble. Asynchronous handoff that works even if you're sleeping during the visit.

**Job 3: The Medication Maze**

"When managing medications for 3+ family members, help me feel like I'm not one forgotten pill away from a medical crisis."

This is the daily-dread job. It's not a crisis — it's the low-grade anxiety of knowing that if you forget one pill, one refill, one interaction, someone could get hurt. Current hires: mental checklists, physical pill organizers, WhatsApp reminders, and hope.

**Success looks like:** zero missed medications without requiring mental tracking.

**Job 4: The Doctor Lottery**

"When I need to find a new doctor for my parent, help me feel confident I'm choosing quality care instead of gambling with my loved one's health."

This job surfaced strongly in research — especially the frustration with unreliable platforms. "Finding a doctor that can be trusted, who doesn't prescribe unnecessary medication... doesn't scare, but treats."

But here's the crucial insight: this job was excluded from the MVP. Not because it isn't real — it is — but because solving it requires a fundamentally different product (recommendation engine, ratings, network effects) that doesn't align with the core visit-cycle hypothesis. We noted it, logged it for V2, and moved on. Saying no to a real job is harder than saying no to a bad idea.

**Job 5: The Sibling Blame Game**

"When coordinating between multiple siblings and family members, help me share the mental load without creating version-control chaos."

The family WhatsApp group is mentioned in almost every interview. It's both the primary coordination tool and the primary source of frustration. "Pressure as the 'default health manager' with no backup." This job, like Job 4, was excluded from MVP scope — it requires multi-user features that would triple the build complexity.

## The MVP Filter: Jobs That Make the Cut

Here's where personas and JTBD become operational. You've got four personas and five jobs. You cannot build for all of them. You must choose.

**The decision matrix:**

| Job | Primary Persona | Frequency | Severity | MVP? | Rationale |
|-----|----------------|-----------|----------|------|-----------|
| J1: 3 AM Emergency | Priya | Low | Critical | No | High stakes but rare; requires comprehensive data already entered — can't be the first experience |
| J2: Remote Visit | Priya, Sandeep | Monthly | High | Yes (core) | The visit cycle (prep → visit → follow-up) is the atomic unit; repeatable, measurable, habitual |
| J3: Medication Maze | Sandeep, Alekhya | Daily | Medium | No (V2) | Important but a different product mode (daily tracking vs. episodic cycle) |
| J4: Doctor Lottery | All | Occasional | High | No (V2) | Requires network/recommendation infrastructure |
| J5: Sibling Blame Game | Priya, Alekhya | Ongoing | Medium | No (V3) | Multi-user complexity; triples the build scope |

One persona (Sandeep). One job (J2 — the visit cycle, which overlaps significantly with J1's information-access need). Eight weeks.

<QuoteBlock quote="Strategy is about making choices, trade-offs; it's about deliberately choosing to be different." attribution="Michael Porter" footnote="6" />

The hardest part wasn't choosing what to build. It was looking at Job 1 — the 3 AM emergency, the most emotionally powerful job in the entire research — and saying "not yet." Because building for the 3 AM emergency first means building a comprehensive health data repository first, which means requiring extensive data entry upfront, which means Sandeep will abandon the app on day one because he doesn't have time.

Instead: build the visit cycle. Each cycle adds health data organically (prescriptions photographed, medication lists built, conditions recorded). After three or four cycles, the 3 AM emergency job solves itself — the data is already there, accumulated through use rather than demanded upfront.

The best product decisions feel like you're building a smaller thing. You're actually building a foundation that makes the bigger things possible.

## Success Criteria (How You Know It Worked)

Every persona needs success criteria. Not product metrics — human outcomes. What does success look like in this person's life?

```text
SUCCESS CRITERIA BY PERSONA
════════════════════════════

SANDEEP:
Before → After
  Anxiety: 6/10 → <3/10
  Visit prep time: 30+ min → <5 min
  Missing documents at doctor: frequent → zero
  Medication tracking: mental → automated
  Post-visit follow-through: sporadic → structured

PRIYA:
Before → After
  Anxiety: 10/10 → <5/10
  Health info access time: "scrolling for ages" → <30 sec
  Post-visit visibility: "I had no idea" → full async review
  Guilt about burdening brother: constant → reduced

ALEKHYA:
Before → After
  Anxiety: 10/10 → <4/10
  Time on health admin: 5+ hours/week → <2 hours
  Apps tried and abandoned: 5-6 → 0 (this one sticks)
  Sleep quality: disrupted → peaceful

RAKESH:
Before → After
  Confidence: "I don't know what I'm doing" → guided
  System in place: none → one
  First doctor visit: terrifying → prepared
```

Notice that every criterion is about the person's experience, not the product's metrics. "50% of users complete 2+ visit cycles" is a product metric — useful for dashboards. "Sandeep's anxiety drops from 6 to 3 and he stops losing sleep" is a success criterion — useful for knowing if you've actually helped someone.

Both matter. But if you have to choose, choose the human one. The product metrics follow.

## The Anti-Persona: Who You're NOT Building For

Just as important as knowing your user is knowing who isn't your user. The anti-persona prevents scope creep by giving you a clear answer when someone says "but what about...?"

```text
ANTI-PERSONAS (WHO THIS PRODUCT IS NOT FOR)
════════════════════════════════════════════

THE DOCTOR
This product doesn't serve healthcare providers.
It serves the people sitting in the waiting room.
If we optimize for the doctor's workflow, we'll
build a medical scribe. That's a different product.

THE HYPOCHONDRIAC GOOGLER
This product doesn't interpret symptoms, suggest
diagnoses, or replace medical consultation. If
someone wants an AI doctor, they'll be disappointed.
The product is coordination, not care.

THE TECH-AVERSE ELDER
The primary user is the caregiver, not the patient.
We don't need Sandeep's parents to download the app.
We need Sandeep to use it on their behalf.

THE DATA MAXIMALIST
Some users want to track everything — weight, steps,
sleep, mood, meals, blood oxygen, heart rate. This
product is not a health dashboard. It's a visit-cycle
tool. Scope discipline is survival.
```
